Indian Festivals

Diwali, also known as the Festival of Lights, is one of the most widely celebrated Hindu festivals in India and across the world. It symbolizes the victory of light over darkness, good over evil, and knowledge over ignorance. The festival usually lasts for five days and falls between October and November, depending on the lunar calendar.

Holi, also known as the Festival of Colors, is a vibrant and joyous Hindu festival celebrated predominantly in India and Nepal, but also across the world. It marks the arrival of spring, the victory of good over evil, and is a celebration of love, unity, and renewal. Holi is typically celebrated in March, on the full moon day of the Hindu month of Phalguna.

Navratri is a significant Hindu festival dedicated to the worship of Goddess Durga and her nine forms, symbolizing the victory of good over evil. The word Navratri means "nine nights" in Sanskrit, and the festival spans over nine days, typically celebrated in the months of September or October, depending on the lunar calendar. It is one of the most widely celebrated festivals in India, with different regions having unique rituals, dances, and traditions.

Ganesh Chaturthi, also known as the Ganesh Festival, is one of the most widely celebrated Hindu festivals in India, dedicated to Lord Ganesha, the elephant-headed deity of wisdom, prosperity, and new beginnings. This vibrant and joyous festival is observed annually and typically lasts for 10 days, starting on the fourth day of the Hindu month of Bhadrapada, which usually falls in August or September.
